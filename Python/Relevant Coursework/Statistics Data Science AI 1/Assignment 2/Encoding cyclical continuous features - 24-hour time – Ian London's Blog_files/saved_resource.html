                      T Bush 4 years ago

                      I've seen this sin/cos transform advice copy/pasted around the internet, but nobody seems to mention (let alone address) its weaknesses:
                      - distance-based algorithms (or loss functions) are distorted in two ways:
                      1) each dimension is usually equally weighted in the distance metric, so splitting into two dimensions effectively double-weights the cyclic feature
                      2) distance between points on the cyclical feature are now a straight line (a chord) instead of an arc, a distortion by a factor of 2x*sin(x/2) where x is the angle between points
                      - it messes up tree-based algorithms that split on a single dimension at a time (as commenter Max noted)

                      Do you have any thoughts on this? Any alternative strategies for cyclical features?

                          I don't know much about this stuff, but I would think that if you are interested in explicitly representing the distance between pairs of inputs, you could just use some kind of modular distance, like min{(b-a)%m, (a-b)%m}, for each coordinate of period m?

                          Also, if the features have to do with time, then the circular representation might make natural sense because the units measure circular motions. E.g. temperature, length of day, and luminance all have an approximate structure in which the top of the circle is at one extreme (noon or summer solstice), the bottom is at the other (midnight or winter solstice), and the marginal changes are fastest in the middle.
